1.5 Key Industry Trends

  • AI & Wearable Analytics: Sports analytics CAGR of 18.5–20.5% — the fastest-growing sub-sector. On-field analysis holds 62%+ share. AI tools optimize nutrition plans, injury prevention, and in-game strategy for elite teams globally.
  • AI Integration (Sony & STATSports): Sony's October 2025 acquisition of STATSports signals major tech-industry convergence with sports analytics. Wearable GPS devices and performance platforms are becoming standard in professional and semi-professional sport.
  • Women's Sports Explosion: Title IX enforcement boosting US youth sport investment; women's athletics, football, and basketball seeing accelerating commercial interest and broadcast deal values.
  • Athleisure Cultural Shift: 65%+ of urban adults globally own functional activewear. 1 in 2 active consumers identify fitness as core to their personal identity. High-fashion tie-ins (Adidas×Balenciaga, Puma×Dua Lipa) expanding sports apparel into mainstream fashion.
  • Esports & Digital Fan Engagement: Virtual and hybrid sports formats offering 24/7 global reach; deeper integration with betting platforms driving both revenue and engagement for broadcasters and leagues.
  • Health Crisis as Opportunity: 31% of adults globally are physically inactive; projected to rise to 35% by 2030 — simultaneously a market risk and a structural opportunity for fitness and wellness sector growth.
  • Geopolitical Sports Investment: Saudi Arabia and Qatar deploying sovereign wealth into football, golf, boxing, and F1 to drive tourism and soft power — reshaping global sports economics in 2024–2026.

2.5 Technology Trends Reshaping Defense

  • AI & Autonomous Systems: $120B+ US DoD R&D allocation with AI as a central focus. 8,500+ US military UAVs and ground robots deployed by 2023. Autonomous weapons — "lethal autonomous weapons systems" (LAWS) — are a subject of intense global debate.
  • Hypersonic Weapons: Hypersonic weapons (capable of Mach 5+) are described as the most disruptive emerging technology in defense. US, China, Russia, and France all have active development programs; first operational systems were deployed in 2022–2025.
  • Drone Warfare Revolution: Ukraine-Russia war proved the decisive battlefield role of cheap commercial drones and loitering munitions ("kamikaze drones"). Demand for counter-drone systems (C-UAS) surged globally. Greece's Centauros anti-drone system proved itself in Red Sea operations 2024–25.
  • Space-Based Defense: Increasing defense investment in satellite communications, ISR (intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance) constellations, and anti-satellite capabilities. Space is formally recognized as a domain of warfare.
  • Cyber Warfare: A $120B+ US R&D allocation included major cybersecurity components. Nation-state cyber attacks on critical infrastructure are now a standard feature of geopolitical competition. Defense budgets now routinely include dedicated cyber commands.
  • Supply Chain Pressures (Tariffs 2025): US tariff increases in spring 2025 surged prices for titanium, carbon fiber composites, and avionics. Defense contractors with fixed-price government contracts absorbed rising costs — a structural financial pressure on the industry.

The ReArm Europe Inflection Point: In early 2025, the EU activated its ReArm Europe / Readiness 2030 program — described as the most significant collective defense investment in European history. Greece became the first EU member to formally use relaxed spending rules under the program to announce a multi-year rearmament package. The bloc agreed to offer €150 billion in low-interest loans to member defense industries, intending to reduce dependence on US-made systems and build an autonomous European defense industrial base. This marks a structural change in European defense economics that will reshape procurement, industrial policy, and military posture for at least a decade.

Strategic Context — Greece vs. Turkey: Greece spends ~3.1% of GDP on defense (2024) — far above the NATO 2% target — because of ongoing disputes with Turkey over the Aegean, eastern Mediterranean EEZ, and Cyprus. Greece has 1,400+ islands, many close to the Turkish coast, requiring extensive military garrisons that are "quite expensive," as analysts note. During the 2010–2018 financial crisis, Greece's defense budget fell to $4.6B — nearly half its current level — while Turkey's economy and defense budget grew substantially. The 2025–2036 plan represents a deliberate strategic catch-up: replacing quantity (where Greece cannot compete with Turkey) with quality and high-tech systems that Turkey does not possess. Greece now positions itself as the EU's southeastern defence anchor and strategic hub for NATO operations in the Eastern Mediterranean, with the Alexandroupoli base serving as a key logistics node for NATO and US forces in Eastern Europe.

  • Mars-Kellanova Mega-Merger ($36B): August 2024's agreement between Mars and Kellanova (Pringles, Pop-Tarts, Cheez-It) represents one of the largest food industry M&A deals in history — signalling continued consolidation pressure in snacks and confectionery.
  • PepsiCo Acquires Poppi ($1.95B): In March 2025, PepsiCo entered the "functional soda" category via acquisition — reflecting a structural shift away from traditional colas toward health-positioned beverages with prebiotics and adaptogens.
  • Plant-Based & Vegan Expansion: Vegan food CAGR of 10.7% through 2034; plant-based protein at 8.7%. However, early market leaders (Beyond Meat) face profitability challenges; the segment is consolidating around viable, differentiated products.
  • Online Grocery Revolution: Food and beverage as % of total US e-commerce grew from 12.8% (2017) to 26%+ (2024), projected 31% by 2029. Global platform-to-consumer food delivery projected at ~$690B in 2025.
  • Supply Chain Vulnerability: Raw material volatility impacts 33% of manufacturers; supply chain inefficiencies affect 29% of operations. Geopolitical disruptions, climate events, and US tariff increases in 2025 created significant cost pressures throughout global food supply chains.

Coca-Cola, Nestlé, and Danone all have major water and carbon reduction commitments tied to regulatory and investor pressure.

Food & Agriculture
Industry in Greece

Greece's food industry is deeply intertwined with its cultural identity, geographic advantages, and agricultural heritage. The Mediterranean climate supports exceptional production of olive oil, feta cheese, fresh fruits, wine, and seafood. Agriculture contributes approximately 4% of Greek GDP — nearly double the EU average — and exports of agricultural products account for one third of total Greek exports. The domestic food market is estimated at $25.8–27.6 billion in 2025, with significant upside from tourism-driven gastronomy and export growth.

3.9 Greek Food Market — Consumer Trends & Challenges

  • Mediterranean Diet Global Premium: Rising global interest in the Mediterranean diet is creating export opportunities for Greek producers. The diet's clinical endorsement for cardiovascular health, longevity, and cognitive function drives demand for Greek olive oil, legumes, nuts, and fresh produce in North American, Northern European, and Asian premium markets.
  • Feta Cheese Price Surge: Feta prices jumped from €7.10/kg (2021) to €12.50/kg (2023) — a 76% increase — driven by input cost inflation and demand. While benefiting producers, the surge risks pricing Greece out of volume markets and into pure premium positioning.
  • Tourism-Food Industry Symbiosis: With tourism generating ~18% of Greek GDP, the food and gastronomy sector benefits from 30M+ annual visitors. The government invested €17M+ from EU Recovery Funds (2022–2025) to develop agro-tourism — opening dairy factories and olive oil mills to tourists as combined gastro-tourism experiences.
  • Organic Farming Boom: Organic farming has grown dramatically in Greece in recent years, partly driven by EU Common Agricultural Policy (CAP 2023–27) incentives (€13.4B strategic plan for Greece approved 2022). Greece's agricultural land is increasingly certified organic, particularly in olive oil, herbs, and vegetables.
  • Plant-Based & Vegan Trend: Growing demand for plant-based and vegan products among Greek consumers, with producers introducing plant-based versions of traditional Greek foods. Weight management product value sales also increased in 2025, reflecting rising health consciousness.
  • Fragmented Production Structure: With approximately 600,000 agricultural workers and small farm sizes (average Greek farm ~5.4 hectares vs. EU average ~17 hectares), Greece faces structural challenges in scaling production, ensuring consistent quality for export, and investing in cold-chain infrastructure.
  • Climate Change Risk: Rising temperatures, increased drought frequency, and extreme weather events threaten olive, grape, and citrus production — the cornerstones of Greek agricultural exports. This is Greece's single largest long-term structural food industry risk.

Greece's Global Food Brand Advantage: Greece punches far above its economic weight in the global food conversation. With approximately €15.5B in food manufacturing and $27.6B in total food market revenue, the country generates outsized global brand value — particularly through olive oil (top-3 global exporter), feta cheese (world's most famous protected cheese by name recognition), Kalamata olives, and the broader Mediterranean diet framework. The EU CAP 2023–2027 strategic plan allocates €13.4B to Greece specifically, creating the foundation for upgrading production capacity, infrastructure, and agro-tourism. The challenge is structural: converting an industry of 20,000+ fragmented SMEs into a competitive, scalable export machine while preserving the artisan quality that commands global premiums.
